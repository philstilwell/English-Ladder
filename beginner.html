📅 May 18, 2026 [0 days, 0 hours old] - US President Calls Off Iran Attack Plans
The US President stopped a planned attack on Iran because new talks are happening.

Beginner ESL: International relations, military actions, and diplomacy

I. The News Brief

The US President is Donald Trump. He talked about Iran. The US planned an attack. The attack was for Tuesday. President Trump called off this attack. Gulf states asked him to stop. They did not want the attack. Serious negotiations are now taking place. President Trump wants to talk. So, the attack did not happen.

II. Vocabulary & Grammar Focus

1. called off (verb phrase): To cancel something that was going to happen.
2. attack (noun): A violent act to hurt or damage something.
3. planned (adjective): Decided beforehand what you are going to do.
4. taking place (verb phrase): To happen or occur.
5. negotiations (noun): Formal talks between groups to reach an agreement.

Grammar Focus: Simple Past Tense
We use the simple past tense to talk about actions that finished in the past. For many verbs, we add '-ed' to the end. For example, 'call' becomes 'called'. Some verbs change completely, like 'is' becomes 'was'.
Example from the text: "President Trump called off this attack."

📅 May 17, 2026 [1 day, 9 hours old] - Ebola Outbreak Declared Global Health Emergency
The World Health Organization says the Ebola outbreak in DR Congo is now a global health emergency.

Beginner ESL: Public Health and Disease Outbreaks

I. The News Brief

The World Health Organization (WHO) helps people stay healthy. They made an important declaration about Ebola. Ebola is a very serious illness that makes people sick. There is an outbreak of Ebola in DR Congo. An outbreak means many people get sick in one area. The WHO now says this is a global health emergency. This means the problem needs help from many countries. About 246 people have caught the illness in DR Congo. Sadly, around 80 people have died because of this sickness. The WHO did not call it a pandemic emergency.

II. Vocabulary & Grammar Focus

1. declaration (noun): an official and important statement
2. outbreak (noun): when a disease suddenly begins to happen to many people at the same time
3. global health emergency (phrase): a very serious health problem that needs urgent action and help from countries all over the world
4. sickness (noun): the state of being ill; a disease or illness
5. pandemic emergency (phrase): a very serious disease that spreads quickly across many countries or continents, causing a widespread health crisis

Grammar Focus: There is / There are
We use 'There is' when we talk about one thing existing. We use 'There are' when we talk about more than one thing existing. It introduces what is present.
Example from the text: "There is an outbreak of Ebola in DR Congo."

📅 May 14, 2026 [4 days, 9 hours old] - US and China Leaders Meet for High-Stakes Talks
Leaders from the US and China are meeting to discuss important topics like trade, Iran, and Taiwan.

Beginner ESL: International Relations and Trade

President Trump arrives in China for very important discussions. These discussions are called high-stakes talks. Trump wants China to open up its markets for more business. 4. trade (noun): the activity of buying, selling, or exchanging goods and services between people or countries.
5. important (adjective): having great value, meaning, or significance; essential.

We use the simple present tense to talk about actions that are planned or scheduled for the future, like a timetable, an official visit, or an important arrival. It shows that the event is definite.

📅 May 13, 2026 [5 days, 8 hours old] - Milka Chocolate Bar Shrinks: A German Court Says 'Guilty' of Shrinkflation
A German court found the Milka chocolate manufacturer guilty because the chocolate bar became smaller but the price stayed the same.

Beginner ESL: Business News and Consumer Rights

I. The News Brief

Milka is a classic chocolate bar from Germany. But recently, the size of this chocolate bar changed. The manufacturer made the bar smaller than before. However, the price of the chocolate stayed the same. Many consumers bought the chocolate and noticed the change. They felt tricked because the bar shrank but the price did not. This change is an example of 'shrinkflation'. A German court in Bremen looked at this problem. The court found the manufacturer guilty of unfair practice. So, the company might need to make the bar bigger again.

II. Vocabulary & Grammar Focus

1. classic (adjective): Something that is very good and popular for a long time.
2. manufacturer (noun): A company or person that makes goods or products.
3. shrank (verb (past simple)): The past form of 'shrink', meaning to become smaller in size.
4. consumers (noun): People who buy and use products and services.
5. guilty (adjective): Responsible for doing something wrong or for a crime.

Grammar Focus: Simple Past Tense
We use the simple past tense to talk about actions that finished at a specific time in the past. Many regular verbs add '-ed' (e.g., looked). Irregular verbs change their form (e.g., 'find' becomes 'found', 'make' becomes 'made', 'shrink' becomes 'shrank').
Example from the text: "The court found the manufacturer guilty of unfair practice."

📅 May 12, 2026 [6 days, 9 hours old] - Trump and Iran: Ceasefire News
The US president talks about a ceasefire between Iran and another group.

Beginner ESL: International Relations and Peace Talks

I. The News Brief

Trump also criticises Iran's idea. Iran offered a counteroffer to stop the war. Trump said the ceasefire is on "massive life support." This means it is very close to ending. He wants the war to end soon.

II. Vocabulary & Grammar Focus

1. ceasefire (noun): A time when people stop fighting in a war.
2. unbelievably weak (adjective phrase): Very, very weak; not strong at all.
3. criticises (verb): To say that you do not like something or someone.
4. counteroffer (noun): A new offer made in response to an old offer.
5. massive life support (noun phrase): A situation where something is barely alive or working, close to ending.

Grammar Focus: Simple Present Tense
We use the simple present tense to talk about things that are always true or happen regularly. We also use it to talk about what people say right now or their current opinions. For 'he/she/it', we often add an 's' to the verb.
Example from the text: "Trump also criticises Iran's idea."
