Adjective Order in English

This rebuilt lesson keeps the original concept image, tightens the structure, and turns the explanation into a clearer self-study guide.

Core Idea

Understanding the order of adjectives is essential for non-native English speakers to construct grammatically correct sentences. The image outlines a general order that adjectives follow when describing a noun. While there is some flexibility, especially in the middle categories, adhering to this order can make your sentences sound more natural.

Key Points

The General Order of Adjectives

  • Determiner
  • Opinion
  • Size
  • Shape
  • Condition
  • Age
  • Color
  • Pattern
  • Origin
  • Material
  • Purpose
  • The Noun

Detailed Explanation with Examples

  • Determiner
    • Words like the, a, an, my, this, that, these, those, some, every, each.
    • Examples:
      • The car
      • My house
      • An apple
  • Opinion
    • Words that express what you think about something, such as beautiful, nice, awful, amazing, ugly.
    • Examples:
      • A beautiful day
      • An awful smell
      • A nice gift
  • Size
    • Words describing the size, such as small, large, tiny, huge.
    • Examples:
      • A large room
      • A tiny insect
      • A small box
  • Shape
    • Words describing the shape, such as round, square, rectangular, oval.
    • Examples:
      • A round table
      • A square garden
      • A rectangular frame
  • Condition
    • Words describing the state or condition, such as broken, messy, clean, hungry, tired.
    • Examples:
      • A broken chair
      • A messy desk
      • A clean window
  • Age
    • Words describing age, such as young, old, new, ancient.
    • Examples:
      • An old tree
      • A young boy
      • A new car
  • Color
    • Words describing color, such as red, blue, green, yellow, black.
    • Examples:
      • A red apple
      • A blue sky
      • A green leaf
  • Pattern
    • Words describing patterns, such as striped, spotted, checked, plain.
    • Examples:
      • A striped shirt
      • A spotted dress
      • A checked tablecloth
  • Origin
    • Words describing the origin, such as American, British, Japanese, Indian.
    • Examples:
      • A British car
      • A Japanese kimono
      • An Indian curry
  • Material
    • Words describing the material, such as wooden, plastic, metal, cotton.
    • Examples:
      • A wooden chair
      • A plastic bottle
      • A metal gate
  • Purpose
    • Words describing the purpose, such as shopping, cleaning, cooking.
    • Examples:
      • A shopping bag
      • A cleaning brush
      • A cooking pot
  • Noun
    • The main noun that you are describing.
    • Examples:
      • The big, round, yellow ball
      • A beautiful, old, wooden table
      • My new, red, Japanese car

Combining Multiple Adjectives

When using multiple adjectives to describe a single noun, follow the order mentioned above.

Example Sentence:

“I have two odd, large, roundish, shiny, new, black and white, checked, French, leather, clown shoes.”

  • Two (determiner)
  • Odd (opinion)
  • Large (size)
  • Roundish (shape)
  • Shiny (condition)
  • New (age)
  • Black and white (color)
  • Checked (pattern)
  • French (origin)
  • Leather (material)
  • Clown (purpose)
  • Shoes (noun)

Note on Flexibility

While the general order is a useful guide, there is some flexibility, especially with the middle categories. The best way to master adjective order is to read extensively in English. Over time, the correct order will start to feel more natural.
