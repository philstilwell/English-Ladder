Families in Venezuela are looking for people after an earthquake.

Beginner ESL: Earthquakes and search efforts in Venezuela.

I. The News Brief

Venezuela had earthquakes recently. Many buildings fell down. Some people are trapped inside the fallen buildings. Families are desperate to find them. They are looking in a city called La Guaira. Families keep vigil near the destroyed homes. They are calling out to their loved ones. They want to move the heavy debris. But moving the rubble is very hard. It is an impossible task for them alone.

II. Vocabulary & Grammar Focus

1. trapped (adjective): Not able to get out of a place.
2. desperate (adjective): Feeling very sad and without hope because of a difficult situation.
3. keep vigil (verb phrase): To stay awake and watch or wait, often at night.
4. heavy debris (noun phrase): Big, broken pieces of things that are difficult to move.
5. rubble (noun): Broken stones and bricks from a destroyed building.

Grammar Focus: Simple Past Tense
We use the Simple Past tense to talk about actions that happened in the past and are now finished. We often add "-ed" to regular verbs. Some verbs are irregular and change completely.
Example from the text: "Venezuela had earthquakes recently."

📅 June 27, 2026 [1 day, 0 hours old] - US Strikes Iran After Cargo Ship Incident
The US and Iran are having problems with military actions against each other.

Beginner ESL: US-Iran Conflict and International Relations

I. The News Brief

The US and Iran have had some problems recently. First, a cargo ship was attacked on the sea. The US believes Iran was involved in this attack. So, the US decided to take military action in response. The US struck targets inside Iran. This happened after the ship attack. Iran said that the US violated an agreement between them. Then, Iran also attacked targets. These targets were linked to American forces in the region. Both countries are now reacting to each other's actions.

II. Vocabulary & Grammar Focus

1. cargo ship (noun phrase): A very large boat that carries goods and materials from one place to another across the sea.
2. military action (noun phrase): The use of an army, navy, or air force to fight or respond to an event.
3. struck targets (verb phrase): Attacked specific places or areas with weapons.
4. violated an agreement (verb phrase): Broke a promise or a formal deal that was made between two or more parties.
5. linked to (adjective phrase): Connected to or associated with something else, showing a relationship.

Grammar Focus: Using 'after' for time order
We use the word 'after' to show that one event happened first, and then another event happened later. It tells us the sequence of actions, meaning what happened next. Event A happens, then AFTER Event A, Event B happens.
Example from the text: "This happened after the ship attack."

📅 June 26, 2026 [1 day, 23 hours old] - Earthquake Strikes Venezuela Amidst Political Uncertainty
A recent earthquake caused significant problems in Venezuela during a time when the country was already facing political difficulties.

Beginner ESL: Natural Disaster and Political Challenges in Venezuela

I. The News Brief

An earthquake hit Venezuela recently. It was a devastating blow for the country. People in Venezuela are now facing uncertainty. This happened when the country was already having problems. Two quakes, called "twin quakes," shook the land. These quakes came less than six months after another big event. Venezuela's then-leader, Nicolás Maduro, was seized. US forces were responsible for taking him. So, Venezuela is dealing with natural disaster and political issues together. The country faces a very unclear future because of these events.

II. Vocabulary & Grammar Focus

1. devastating blow (noun phrase): a very bad event that causes great damage or sadness for a country.
2. uncertainty (noun): a situation where you do not know what will happen next; a feeling of being unsure.
3. twin quakes (noun phrase): two earthquakes that happen very close together in time or location.
4. then-leader (noun): a person who was the main person in charge of a country at a specific time in the past.
5. seized (verb): taken by force or official power, often suddenly and without warning.

Grammar Focus: Simple Past Tense (Passive Voice)
We use the Simple Past tense to talk about actions that finished in the past. When the subject of the sentence receives the action, we use the passive voice. We form it with 'was' or 'were' and the past participle form of the verb. For example, 'was seized' means someone else performed the action of seizing.
Example from the text: "Venezuela's then-leader, Nicolás Maduro, was seized."

📅 June 24, 2026 [3 days, 23 hours old] - France Experiences Power Outages During Heatwave
Many homes in France lost electricity because of very hot weather.

Beginner ESL: Extreme Weather and Electricity Problems

I. The News Brief

France is having very hot weather right now. This hot weather is a record heatwave across the country. A heatwave means it is very hot for many days, which can cause problems. Because of the heatwave, there are now power outages in some areas. Power outages mean that homes do not have electricity to use. About 68,000 homes in Brittany were affected by these outages. Brittany is a region in France that is experiencing the extreme heat. Many people in Brittany suddenly lost their electricity supply. The electricity might not be fully restored very quickly for everyone. It may take until Wednesday night for all affected homes to get power back.

II. Vocabulary & Grammar Focus

1. heatwave (noun): A period of many days when the weather is extremely hot.
2. power outages (noun phrase): Times when the electricity supply stops, so there is no power in homes or buildings.
3. affected (verb (past participle)): Had a problem or change because of something, like losing power.
4. electricity (noun): The power that makes lights, televisions, and other machines work.
5. restored (verb (past participle)): Brought back to its normal or original state, like getting power back.

Grammar Focus: Passive Voice in Simple Past
We use the passive voice to talk about an action when we do not know who did it, or when the action itself is more important than who did it. We form it with 'was' or 'were' + a past participle verb.
Example from the text: "About 68,000 homes in Brittany were affected by these outages."
